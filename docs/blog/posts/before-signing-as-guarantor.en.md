# Before You Sign as Guarantor: Risks Most Borrowers and Families Overlook

People often sign as guarantors in moments of trust. A relative asks for help. A family business needs support. A bank official says it is a routine formality. The signature is given quickly, sometimes without even keeping a copy. That is one of the most common legal mistakes in lending transactions.

Under Section 128 of the Indian Contract Act, the liability of the surety is co-extensive with that of the principal debtor unless the contract provides otherwise. In plain terms, this means the guarantor's exposure can be substantial. A guarantee is not a ceremonial endorsement. It is a legal undertaking with serious financial consequences. ([India Code][1])

The first risk people overlook is scope. Many guarantors do not actually know what facility they are guaranteeing. Is it a one-time term loan, a cash credit arrangement, a continuing facility, or a set of obligations that may change over time? Without understanding the facility, the guarantor cannot realistically understand the risk.

Second, the guarantee document itself matters. Some guarantees are limited. Others are continuing. Some are linked to a specific transaction. Others are drafted broadly enough to create wider exposure than the guarantor expects. Signing without reading is not caution. It is surrender.

Third, guarantors often assume that the bank must first exhaust every remedy against the borrower. That assumption can be dangerous. The existence of a principal borrower does not make the guarantor immune. In secured lending, property-based enforcement rights may also exist under the SARFAESI framework. A family member who signs casually may later discover that both contractual and security-based consequences have become active at the same time. ([India Code][2])

Fourth, many families ignore the importance of documentation. Before signing, a guarantor should insist on seeing the sanction terms, amount, purpose of facility, security structure, repayment framework, and key default consequences. After signing, a copy should always be retained. Memory is a weak defence in a document-heavy dispute.

Fifth, later developments matter. Renewals, restructuring, additional advances, and modified terms can all become important depending on the contract and the record. A guarantor who never asks what happened after the initial signing may be taking a larger risk than intended.

This does not mean nobody should ever become a guarantor. It means the decision should be informed, documented, and deliberate. The right question is not whether you trust the borrower. The right question is whether you understand the legal exposure you are assuming.

The safest practical rule is simple: read before signing, ask for copies, understand the exact facility, check whether security already exists, and do not sign merely because the atmosphere feels urgent.

In guarantee matters, hesitation before signing is often wiser than regret after default.
