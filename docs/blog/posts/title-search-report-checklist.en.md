# What a Proper Title Search Report Should Actually Cover Before Buying Property

Clients often ask for a title search as though it were a single document that can be produced by glancing at a sale deed and a revenue extract. A proper title search is much more than that. It is a structured legal examination of how title is claimed, how it has moved, whether it is consistent, and whether any visible risk emerges from the record.

The first thing a title search report should examine is the root of title. Who claims ownership today is only one part of the story. A sound report should ask how that ownership is derived and whether the chain is complete. Missing links, unexplained transfers, unclear inheritance, defective descriptions, and inconsistent boundaries are all warning signs.

Second, the report should evaluate continuity. A title chain is not strong merely because documents exist. The documents must also align with each other. Dates, executants, property particulars, area, khata or khasra details where relevant, survey references, mutation trail, and supporting revenue or municipal entries should be checked for consistency.

Third, encumbrances matter. Mortgages, charges, attachments, court disputes, acquisition issues, restrictive orders, family claims, and prior agreements can all affect the practical quality of title. In mortgage-linked matters, CERSAI's public database function under the SARFAESI framework underscores the value of disciplined security and encumbrance checking. ([India Code][1]) ([CERSAI][2])

Fourth, possession and paper title should not be confused. A person in possession is not necessarily a person with clear legal title. Likewise, neat paperwork is not always conclusive if there are visible gaps in possession history, access, litigation exposure, or succession claims.

Fifth, the report should identify transaction-specific concerns. A lender examining property for mortgage may focus on enforceability and marketability of security. A buyer may focus on clean transferability and future litigation risk. A family settlement property may require a different kind of caution than a builder transaction or an agricultural parcel.

In mortgage law, the Transfer of Property Act treats a mortgage as transfer of an interest in specific immovable property to secure money advanced or to be advanced. It also recognises the mortgagor's right of redemption once the money becomes due and is paid. These are not just statutory definitions. They underline why the underlying title must be examined with care before any loan or purchase is treated as safe. ([India Code][3]) ([India Code][4])

A good title report should therefore do more than say that documents were seen. It should identify the documents reviewed, state the period of search, note the visible chain, flag gaps, comment on encumbrance position to the extent examined, and clearly state any assumptions or limitations. A vague title opinion may be more dangerous than no title opinion at all.

Property disputes are expensive because they grow from small omissions. A proper title search report reduces avoidable risk by identifying those omissions before money changes hands.

That is why title scrutiny should be treated as preventive legal work, not post-facto paperwork.
