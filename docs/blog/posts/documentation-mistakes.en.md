In banking and recovery work, documents do not merely support a case. Very often, they become the case.

Many lending disputes do not begin with a dramatic default. They begin much earlier, at the stage of sanction, execution, record-keeping, or follow-up. By the time an account turns irregular or a recovery step becomes necessary, the real difficulty is often not the borrower's refusal alone. It is the discovery that the paperwork is incomplete, inconsistent, or poorly preserved.

One recurring problem is mismatch between the sanction terms and the execution set. The sanction may reflect one set of terms while the loan agreement, guarantee, security creation papers, or disbursement trail reflect another. Even when the underlying transaction is genuine, weak documentary alignment creates room for avoidable dispute. In contentious matters, small inconsistencies are rarely treated as small.

A second common issue is weak security documentation. In matters involving immovable property, a mortgage is legally understood as a transfer of an interest in specific immovable property to secure payment of money, and the mortgagor also retains a statutory right of redemption once the secured money becomes due and is paid. That makes clarity of title papers, mortgage creation records, and post-creation documentation especially important. ([India Code][2])

Third, guarantee documentation is often treated too casually. In practice, many people assume the guarantee is secondary in importance because the borrower is the principal obligor. Legally, however, Section 128 of the Indian Contract Act states that the surety's liability is co-extensive with that of the principal debtor unless the contract provides otherwise. That is precisely why guarantee wording, execution quality, and preservation of the record matter so much. ([India Code][3])

Another frequent weakness lies in the account trail itself. Once an account becomes stressed, the bank's future action depends heavily on internal accuracy: statements, recall basis, classification trail, communications, and proof of service. RBI material aimed at the public explains the widely used 90-day overdue benchmark for NPA classification in many account categories. Once an account crosses into enforcement territory, documentation quality becomes even more important because the SARFAESI Act allows a secured creditor to enforce security interest without intervention of court or tribunal in the manner provided by the statute. ([Reserve Bank of India][4])

The practical lesson is simple. Good documentation does not guarantee a dispute-free account, but poor documentation almost always makes a bad situation worse. Clean execution, consistent records, timely follow-up, and careful custody of documents are not merely back-office tasks. They are preventive legal strategy.

For institutions, the answer is periodic documentation audits and disciplined record review. For borrowers and guarantors, the answer is equally important: understand what you are signing, keep a copy, and do not assume missing details will never matter later.
