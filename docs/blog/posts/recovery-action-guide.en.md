Few areas of banking law are surrounded by more misunderstanding than the relationship between borrowers, guarantors, and recovery action.

Many guarantors sign as a gesture of support, assuming their role is only symbolic. Many borrowers believe recovery begins only when a case is filed in court. Both assumptions are often wrong.

Under Section 128 of the Indian Contract Act, the liability of the surety is co-extensive with that of the principal debtor unless the contract provides otherwise. In practical terms, that means a guarantor's liability can be very real and very serious. A guarantee is not a ceremonial signature. It is a legal commitment. ([India Code][3])

Borrowers and guarantors should also understand that recovery action is not a single event. It is usually a process. It may involve reminders, default notices, classification consequences, recall action, negotiation, enforcement steps, civil proceedings, cheque dishonour litigation, or action under a security framework depending on the nature of the transaction and the documents in place.

Where a cheque has been issued and dishonoured for insufficiency of funds or related reasons, Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act may come into play, and the Act also contains a statutory presumption in favour of the holder under Section 139, along with provisions on cognizance in Section 142. These are important provisions, but they do not eliminate the need for careful factual and documentary analysis in every case. ([India Code][7])

Where secured assets are involved, the SARFAESI Act provides that a security interest in favour of a secured creditor may be enforced without the intervention of court or tribunal in the manner provided by the Act. That is why borrowers and guarantors should take notices seriously and seek advice early rather than after positions harden. ([India Code][8])

At the same time, parties should remember that rights exist on both sides. In mortgage matters, the Transfer of Property Act recognises the mortgagor's right of redemption once the secured amount has become due and is paid. The legal landscape is therefore not one-sided. Duties, liabilities, and remedies operate together. ([India Code][9])

The most useful practical advice is straightforward:
read before signing, keep copies of all documents, do not ignore notices, do not rely on oral assurances where written obligations exist, and do not assume that being "only a guarantor" means being legally safe.

For lenders, careful documentation and consistent follow-up remain essential. For borrowers and guarantors, early clarity is the best protection. Most recovery problems become harder, not easier, when they are left unattended.
