# Can a Bank Proceed Against the Guarantor and the Mortgaged Property at the Same Time?

This is one of the most common questions in banking disputes. Borrowers often assume that the bank must first exhaust its remedies against the principal borrower. Guarantors often assume that they are exposed only after every other step has failed. In practice, neither assumption is safe.

Under Section 128 of the Indian Contract Act, the liability of the surety is co-extensive with that of the principal debtor unless the contract provides otherwise. That is a short provision with very significant consequences. It means that a guarantee is not merely symbolic support. It is a real legal obligation. ([India Code][1])

Where a loan is secured by mortgage or other security interest, the legal structure may involve more than one layer of protection for the lender. There may be a principal borrower, a guarantor, and secured property. From the borrower's perspective, these may feel like separate compartments. From the lender's perspective, they are often part of the same recovery architecture.

This is why many disputes arise when guarantors say that the bank should first sell the mortgaged property and only then proceed against them. The problem with that argument is that the guarantee and the security are not always sequential remedies in the way laypersons imagine. Much depends on the contract documents, the nature of the facility, and the enforcement mechanism involved.

In secured transactions, Section 13 of the SARFAESI Act allows enforcement of security interest without intervention of court or tribunal in the manner provided by the Act. At the same time, the guarantor's contractual liability continues to matter. The existence of one remedy does not automatically neutralise the other. ([India Code][2])

That does not mean banks can proceed carelessly. Documentation, service of notices, computation of dues, description of security, and compliance with the governing framework remain important. But as a matter of legal understanding, a guarantor should never assume that being "only a guarantor" provides meaningful insulation.

Another point is often overlooked. The Transfer of Property Act recognises the mortgagor's right of redemption once the secured amount becomes due and is paid. This means property enforcement is not simply a mechanical event. Rights, remedies, and obligations move together. A person involved in the transaction must look at the entire legal position, not one isolated document. ([India Code][3])

The practical takeaway is simple. If you are a guarantor, read the guarantee deed before signing and keep a copy. Understand whether it is limited or continuing, what the facility covers, whether other securities exist, and whether any later variation has taken place. If you are already facing recovery action, gather the sanction papers, guarantee, mortgage record, account statements, and correspondence before forming a legal strategy.

In many disputes, the real issue is not whether the bank can ever proceed against both the guarantor and the secured asset. The real issue is whether the transaction documents and the recovery record support the action actually taken.

Strong legal advice in such matters begins with documents, not assumptions.
