When clients hear the phrase "title search," they often assume it is a routine paperwork exercise. In reality, title scrutiny is one of the most important risk-control steps in any property transaction, mortgage creation, or loan backed by immovable property.

Property disputes are rarely caused by one dramatic defect. More often, they arise because an old defect was missed, a document was assumed to be enough when it was not, or the chain was never examined with proper care. The result may be delayed finance, compromised security, costly litigation, or a transaction that looks complete on paper but remains legally vulnerable.

Under the Transfer of Property Act, a mortgage is the transfer of an interest in specific immovable property for the purpose of securing money advanced or to be advanced. The same statute also recognises the mortgagor's right of redemption once the secured amount becomes due and is paid. These are not abstract concepts. They underscore why the underlying title and security record must be sound from the outset. ([India Code][2])

A proper title review is not limited to checking who currently claims ownership. It asks more difficult questions. How did the present owner derive title? Is the chain continuous? Are there missing links? Does the description of property remain consistent through the record? Are there pending disputes, acquisition issues, family claims, succession questions, or signs of prior encumbrance? Are the revenue, municipal, and supporting records aligned with the transaction set?

For lenders, title scrutiny serves a dual purpose. It helps assess whether the borrower has valid rights in the property and whether the proposed security is practically enforceable. For buyers, it helps answer an equally important question: am I purchasing a legally defensible title, or merely possession backed by incomplete paperwork?

In modern secured transactions, another practical layer is registration of security interest data. CERSAI states that the object of the registration system under Chapter IV of the SARFAESI Act is to create a public database of encumbrances created on properties. That makes search and verification discipline even more valuable in mortgage-linked matters. ([CERSAI][6])

Good title scrutiny does not promise that a property will never become disputed. What it does is reduce avoidable risk. It identifies gaps before money changes hands, before a mortgage is created, and before a dispute becomes expensive.

The most common mistake clients make is waiting to seek legal review until the transaction is already emotionally or financially committed. By then, legal advice becomes damage control. The better stage is earlier, when the transaction can still be structured properly, clarified, or avoided.

In property work, prevention is almost always less costly than litigation. Title scrutiny is where that prevention begins.
